WHAT ARE ALL THOSE SIDECHAINS DOING?

A protein chain is under many, competing constraints that give rise to structure.

Thesis: The backbone shrinks away from water (hydrophobic effect)...
Antithesis: but must to find hydrogen bond partners.
Synthesis: This leads to secondary structures like $\alpha$-helices and $\beta$-sheets

A protein chain is under many, competing constraints that give rise to structure.

Thesis: Hydrophobic amino acids collapse together...
Antithesis: but have to find a way to do so that doesn't leave a "hole"
Synthesis: This leads to a hydrophobic "core" in most proteins.

A protein chain is under many, competing constraints that give rise to structure.

Thesis: Charged and polar amino acids must form polar interactions...
Antithesis: but this requires specific geometries between amino acids
Synthesis: This gives the protein a specific shape.

A protein chain is under many, competing constraints that give rise to structure.

Thesis: Proline and Glycine disrupt secondary structure...
Antithesis: but their neighbors in the protein sequence will strongly prefer secondary structure.
Synthesis: This leads to turns that allow the protein to fold back on itself and form a compact shape.

Key point: Different amino acid sequences will have different constraints and thus different folds

The structure that fulfills the most constraints (and thus has the lowest $\Delta G$) is the native state of the protein

Things can be very complicated

Alexander et al. (2009) Proceedings of the National Academy of Science 106(50):21149-21154

The free energy of folding ($\Delta G_{fold}$) is usually:

$\approx -20$ to $-40\ kJ \cdot mol^{-1}$

This is about the strength of a single hydrogen bond!

Why might this be?

Proteins are marginally stable because:

  • It is hard to keep optimizing each constraint without compromising others
  • It's biologically reasonable to make such structures transient
  • Evolution only makes them stable "enough" but can't optimize further

Summary I

  • Protein folding requires satisfying many constraints for each residue in the amino acid chain
  • The native state is the structure that fulfills the most constraints simultaneously
  • This native state is marginally stable ($\approx -20$ to $-40\ kJ \cdot mol^{-1}$)
  • The "rules" of protein complicated and not fully worked out

How to proteins recognize specific molecules in their environment for function?

The estrogen receptor binds estradiol in female metazoans

Two key ideas:

Specificity: proteins can discriminate amongst targets in a cell

Affinity: to sense changes in target concentration, the affinity of the protein for the target must match changes in target concentration

Recognition occurs because the pocket has the right shape

BUT, the estrogen receptor can also discriminate these two hormones

estradiol (estrogen)

testosterone

How is this achieved?

The hydrophobic effect tends to favor binding all the time

Polar contacts tend to have little favorable effect on binding

But mismatched polar contacts strongly disfavor binding

Summary II

  • Proteins can bind to specific molecules in the cellular environment
  • Interactions are driven by the hydrophobic effect, but specificity is achieved by shape complementarity and polar contacts
  • The strength of these interactions is quantified by the Dissociation constant ($K_{D}$)
  • $K_{D}$ is tuned to the concentration of the molecule in the cell
